\title[Testing for Simplicial Geometry]{Testing for Simplicial Geometry: A Signed Triangle
  Statistic for the \v{C}ech--2PC Phase Transition}

\author[Goh and Cook]{David Goh}
\address{Department of Computer Science, University of Toronto}
\email{daveed@cs.toronto.edu}

\author{Nicholas A.\ Cook}
\address{Department of Mathematics, Duke University}

\keywords{random simplicial complex, geometry detection, Čech complex, signed triangle statistic,
  phase transition, Lean 4, formal verification}

\begin{abstract}
Random simplicial complexes are natural higher-dimensional analogues of random graphs, arising
in topological data analysis and the study of networks with higher-order structure. A basic
question in statistical inference on such objects is whether an observed complex reflects
latent geometric organization---in which the vertices have positions in some ambient space
that drive the connection structure---or whether its triangle structure appeared by chance,
with no underlying geometry. We study a precise formulation of this geometry detection
problem: given an observed 2-dimensional simplicial complex on $n$ vertices, determine whether
it arose from the \emph{2-parameter complex} $\PC(n,p,q)$---in which edges and triangle fills
are independent---or from the \v{C}ech complex of $n$ points sampled uniformly on the flat
torus $\TT^d$, with radius chosen to match the edge probability~$p$.

We introduce the \emph{doubly-signed filled triangle statistic} $\tauf$, centered with respect
to both edge and fill indicators. Under $\PC$, $\tauf$ has mean zero and variance $\Theta(n^3)$.
Under the \v{C}ech model, $\tauf$ has mean $\binom{n}{3}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)$, where
$\geomCov(p,d)\to 0$ as $d\to\infty$. There is an explicit sharp threshold
$d^*(p) = |\log p|/\log(3/2)$: for $d \geq d^*(p)$, every triple of points on $\TT^d$ fills
and $\geomCov(p,d) = 0$ \emph{exactly}, so detection is impossible; for $d < d^*(p)$,
$\geomCov(p,d) > 0$ and detection succeeds when $n^{3/2}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty$.
All moment results, detection lower bound, the phase transition $\geomCov(p,d)=0$ for $d\geq d^*(p)$,
and the limit $\geomCov(p,d)\to 0$ are formally verified in Lean~4 with
Mathlib~\cite{Mathlib2020}, with no remaining sorries in the active proof chain.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Motivation and Background}

Topological data analysis (TDA) infers the shape of data by building simplicial complexes on
point clouds and measuring their homology. The foundational pipeline---sample $n$ points, form
the \v{C}ech or Vietoris-Rips complex at radius $r$, read off Betti numbers or persistent
homology---rests on the assumption that the observed topological structure reflects genuine
geometry in the underlying data. But any finite point cloud will produce some triangles and
higher faces by chance; the practical question is whether those topological features are
signal or noise.

Kahle~\cite{Kahle2011} systematically studied the expected topology of \v{C}ech and
Vietoris-Rips complexes built on i.i.d.\ random points in $\RR^d$, establishing connectivity
and component-count thresholds as higher-dimensional analogues of results for random geometric
graphs. As Kahle notes, a key motivation is ``the need for a probabilistic null hypothesis
to compare with topological statistics of point cloud data''~\cite{Kahle2011}. The survey of
Bobrowski and Kahle~\cite{Bobrowski2018} makes this connection precise: studying the topology
of random geometric complexes ``is related to developing probabilistic null hypotheses for
topological statistics,'' and the random geometric complex on i.i.d.\ uniform points is the
natural geometric model against which TDA test statistics should be calibrated.

The simplest null model for 2-dimensional complexes is one with \emph{no} underlying geometry:
insert each edge independently with probability $p$ and fill each available triangle
independently with probability $q$. This is the \emph{2-parameter complex} $\PC(n,p,q)$
(see also the multi-parameter model of Kahle~\cite{Kahle2016}).
The fundamental inference question is then: given only the observed simplicial complex,
can one distinguish the structured geometric model from the purely random null?

\subsection{The Detection Problem}

Networks in the real world often exhibit higher-order structure: not just pairwise connections
between entities, but groups of three or more that are jointly related. Simplicial complexes
formalize this by augmenting a graph with filled triangles, tetrahedra, and higher faces.
Random simplicial complexes serve as null models for such data, and a natural question arises:
\emph{does an observed complex carry latent geometric structure, or did it arise by a purely
combinatorial chance process?}

To make this precise, we focus on 2-dimensional random simplicial complexes---those with
vertices, edges, and filled triangles. The simplest null model is the
\emph{2-parameter complex} $\PC(n,p,q)$: place $n$ vertices, include each edge independently
with probability $p$, and fill each completed triangle independently with probability $q$.
This is a maximally unstructured model---edge and fill events are all mutually independent,
encoding no geometry whatsoever.

The alternative is a \emph{geometric} model. Sample $n$ points uniformly on the flat torus
$\TT^d$; connect two points by an edge whenever they lie within distance $r$; fill a triangle
whenever all three associated balls of radius $r$ have a common point (the nerve criterion).
This is the \v{C}ech complex $\Cech(n,r,d)$, and its triangle structure is governed by the
ambient positions of the points, not by independent coin flips. The detection question is
whether, given only the simplicial complex, one can tell which model generated it.

This type of question has been studied in the graph setting by Bubeck, Ding, Eldan, and
R\'acz~\cite{Bubeck2016}, who established a sharp phase transition: random geometric graphs
on $\Sph^{d-1}$ are distinguishable from Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi graphs $G(n,p)$ when
$d = O(n^3/\log n)$, and indistinguishable when $d\gg n^3$. Their key tool was a signed
triangle statistic that exploits the fact that edges sharing a vertex are positively correlated
under the geometric model. Our work carries this investigation into the simplicial setting,
where the presence of fill indicators introduces an additional layer of geometric dependence
beyond what edge structure alone can capture.

\subsection{Main Results}

We state our results informally here; formal statements appear in
Sections~\ref{sec:statistic} and~\ref{sec:detection}.

\begin{theorem}[Informal]\label{thm:informal}
Fix $p\in(0,1)$ and let $\tauf$ be the doubly-signed statistic with matched parameters.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item \textbf{Detection} (Theorem~\ref{thm:detection})\textbf{.}
  If $n^{3/2}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty$, the test that thresholds $\tauf$
  achieves asymptotic power $1$: $\TV(\PC,\Cech)\to 1$.

\item \textbf{Phase transition via critical dimension} (Theorem~\ref{thm:phase-transition})\textbf{.}
  There is a critical dimension $d^*(p) = |\log p|/\log(3/2)\approx 2.47\,|\log p|$:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Detectable:} For $d < d^*(p)$, $\geomCov(p,d)>0$ and
      detection succeeds when $n^{3/2}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty$.
    \item \textbf{Collapsed:} For $d\geq d^*(p)$, $\geomCov(p,d)=0$ exactly
      and no test can distinguish the models.
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item \textbf{The doubly-signed statistic.} We define
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:tauf}
    \tauf = \sum_{\{i,j,k\}\in\binom{[n]}{3}}
      \Bigl[\prod_{e\in\partial\{i,j,k\}}(A_e - p)\Bigr](F_{ijk} - q),
  \end{equation}
  a statistic centered with respect to both edge and fill variability. This is the natural
  simplicial analogue of the BDER signed triangle statistic.

\item \textbf{Moment computations.} We prove (Propositions~\ref{prop:moments-twopc}
  and~\ref{prop:moments-cech}) that under $\PC(n,p,q)$,
  $\EE[\tauf] = 0$ and $\Var[\tauf] = \binom{n}{3}p^3(1-p)^3 q(1-q)$. Under
  $\Cech(n,r,d)$ with matched parameters, $\EE[\tauf] = \binom{n}{3}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)$.

\item \textbf{Phase transition.} We identify the explicit critical dimension
  $d^*(p) = |\log p|/\log(3/2)$ (Definition~\ref{def:critical-dim}): for $d \geq d^*(p)$
  the matched ball radius exceeds $1/3$, every triple fills by a 1D Helly argument, and
  $\geomCov(p,d) = 0$ exactly. For $d < d^*(p)$, the SNR condition
  $n^{3/2}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty$ drives detection.

\item \textbf{Lean verification.} The moment results, detection lower bound, phase
  transition (both regimes), and $\geomCov(p,d) \to 0$ are formally verified in Lean~4
  with Mathlib, with no remaining sorries in the active proof chain;
  see Appendix~\ref{app:lean} for the full catalog of verified results.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Comparison with BDER}\label{sec:bder-comparison}

The BDER problem compares $G(n,p)$ with the random geometric graph $\RGG(n,p,d)$ on
$\Sph^{d-1}$. Their key tool is the \emph{signed triangle statistic}
\[
  T_{\mathrm{BDER}} = \sum_{\{i,j,k\}\in\tbinom{[n]}{3}}
    (A_{ij}-p)(A_{ik}-p)(A_{jk}-p),
\]
which exploits the positive correlation of edges sharing a vertex under the geometric model.
The quantity driving detection is the pairwise geometric covariance
\[
  \mathrm{cov}_{ij,ik}
  = \mathrm{Cov}_{\RGG}(A_{ij}, A_{ik})
  = \EE_{\RGG}\!\bigl[(A_{ij}-p)(A_{ik}-p)\bigr].
\]
Under $\RGG$, $\mathrm{cov}_{ij,ik} > 0$; under $G(n,p)$, $\mathrm{cov}_{ij,ik} = 0$ by
independence.

In our setting the analogous structure requires a second layer of centering. Even if the
edge indicators are correlated, the fill indicator $F_{ijk}$ carries additional geometric
information: under the \v{C}ech model, the fill event depends on all three points
simultaneously. Our doubly-signed statistic (Definition~\ref{def:tauf}) augments
$T_{\mathrm{BDER}}$ by a centered fill factor:
\[
  \tauf = \sum_{\{i,j,k\}}(A_{ij}-p)(A_{ik}-p)(A_{jk}-p)\cdot(F_{ijk}-q).
\]
The corresponding per-triangle contribution is
\[
  \geomCov(p,d)
  = \EE_{\Cech}\!\bigl[(A_{ij}-p)(A_{ik}-p)(A_{jk}-p)(F_{ijk}-q)\bigr]
\]
(Definition~\ref{def:geomcov}), the simplicial analogue of $\mathrm{cov}_{ij,ik}$.

The detection threshold in BDER is $d^*\asymp n^3$ (growing with $n$). Our threshold
$d^*(p) = |\log p|/\log(3/2)$ is \emph{finite and independent of $n$}: above it,
$\geomCov(p,d) = 0$ exactly and detection is impossible regardless of sample size.
This sharp collapse---rather than a gradual SNR decay---is the distinctive feature of
the simplicial setting.

The fill layer introduces richer geometric dependence than edge structure alone. We conjecture
that this additional topological information is conducive to test statistics with greater power
than in the BDER setting: the doubly-signed statistic exploits two layers of centering, and we
expect the detection condition $n^{3/2}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty$ to be achievable at
tighter sample bounds than the analogous BDER condition for comparable $(p,d)$, reflecting
the stronger geometric signal encoded in simplicial fills.

\subsection{Related Work}

\paragraph{Geometry testing for random graphs.}
Bubeck, Ding, Eldan, and R\'acz~\cite{Bubeck2016} introduced the signed triangle statistic
for testing $G(n,p)$ vs.\ $\RGG(n,p,d)$ on $\Sph^{d-1}$, establishing the $d\asymp n^3$
phase transition. Bangachev and Bresler~\cite{BangachevBresler2024} studied the low-degree
Fourier coefficients of random geometric graphs on $\Sph^{d-1}$, showing that the signed
triangle statistic is computationally optimal for this testing problem and characterizing the
Fourier coefficient behavior across the full range of densities. Liu, Mohanty, Schramm, and
Yang~\cite{Liu2022} obtained sharp detection thresholds for sparse random geometric graphs
via the low-degree polynomial framework; their methods provide a model for sharpening the
SNR condition below our threshold $d^*(p)$.

\paragraph{Hypergraph detection.}
Dhawan, Mao, and Wein~\cite{DhawanMaoWein2023} study detection of planted dense
subhypergraphs via low-degree polynomials in $r$-uniform hypergraphs, establishing sharp
thresholds in the log-density regime. Their setting is combinatorial (planted structure in an
Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi null) rather than geometric, but the low-degree framework and the
connection between hyperedge counting statistics and the structure of test polynomials is
closely related to our context.

\paragraph{Random geometric complexes and TDA.}
Kahle~\cite{Kahle2011} established homology thresholds for \v{C}ech and Vietoris-Rips
complexes on random points in $\RR^d$, motivated by the need for null models in TDA.
Kahle~\cite{Kahle2016} surveys the multi-parameter model $X(n;p_1,p_2,\ldots)$ (of which
our $\PC(n,p,q)$ is a special case) and the connection to persistent homology.
Bobrowski and Kahle~\cite{Bobrowski2018} survey random geometric complexes, explicitly
connecting the topology of such complexes to null hypotheses for topological statistics.
Tem\v{c}inas, Nanda, and Reinert~\cite{Temcinas2025} develop goodness-of-fit tests for
random simplicial complexes via CLTs for subcomplex counts.

\section{Models and Parameter Matching}\label{sec:models}

\subsection{Two Random Models of Simplicial Complexes}

\begin{definition}[2-parameter complex]\label{def:twopc}
The \emph{2-parameter complex} $\PC(n,p,q)$ is the random 2-dimensional simplicial complex
on vertex set $[n] = \{1,\ldots,n\}$ constructed as follows: each edge $\{i,j\}$ is included
independently with probability $p\in(0,1)$; each 3-clique $\{i,j,k\}$ (three vertices with
all three edges present) is filled to a 2-simplex independently with probability $q\in(0,1)$.
\end{definition}

The 2PC is the natural null model for random simplicial complexes: it places filled triangles
at each available 3-cycle with no imposed geometry. Under $\PC$, edge indicators $A_{ij}$
and fill indicators $F_{ijk}$ are all mutually independent.

\begin{definition}[Flat torus]\label{def:torus}
Let $d\geq 1$. The \emph{flat torus} $\TT^d$ is the product space $(\RR/\ZZ)^d$ equipped
with the sup-norm metric
\[
  d_\infty(x,y) = \max_{i\leq d}\lvert x_i - y_i\rvert_\ZZ,
  \quad \lvert t\rvert_\ZZ = \min_{k\in\ZZ}\lvert t - k\rvert.
\]
Under this metric, the ball of radius $r$ centered at any point has volume $(2r)^d$,
provided $r \leq 1/2$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[\v{C}ech complex]\label{def:cech}
The \emph{\v{C}ech complex} $\Cech(n,r,d)$ is constructed by sampling $n$ points
$x_1,\ldots,x_n$ uniformly and independently on $\TT^d$. The edge $\{i,j\}$ is included
when $d_\infty(x_i,x_j)\leq r$. The triangle $\{i,j,k\}$ is filled when all three edges are
present and the balls $B(x_i,r)$, $B(x_j,r)$, $B(x_k,r)$ have nonempty common intersection
(the nerve criterion):
\[
  F_{ijk} = \mathbf{1}\!\left[A_{ij}A_{ik}A_{jk} = 1\right]\cdot
  \mathbf{1}\!\left[\bigcap_{\ell\in\{i,j,k\}} B(x_\ell,r) \neq \emptyset\right].
\]
\end{definition}

\subsection{Parameter Matching}

To compare $\PC$ and $\Cech$ on equal footing, we match the marginal edge probability $p$
and the marginal fill probability $q$. Recall that
$\TV(\mu,\nu) = \sup_{A}|\mu(A)-\nu(A)|$ denotes the total variation distance.

\begin{definition}[matchRadius]\label{def:matchradius}
Given $p\in(0,1)$ and $d\geq 1$, the \emph{matched radius} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:matchradius}
  r(p,d) = \frac{p^{1/d}}{2}.
\end{equation}
This is the unique $r\in(0,1/2]$ satisfying $(2r)^d = p$, i.e., the sup-norm ball of radius
$r$ has volume exactly $p$.
\end{definition}

With this choice, points $x_i, x_j$ sampled uniformly on $\TT^d$ satisfy
$\PP[d_\infty(x_i,x_j)\leq r] = p$. The formula~\eqref{eq:matchradius} is formally verified
in Lean as \lean{matchRadius\_spec} \leanverified{matchRadius\_spec}.

\begin{definition}[fillingProb]\label{def:fillingprob}
Let $r = r(p,d)$. The \emph{filling probability} is
\[
  \fillingP(p,d)
  = \PP\bigl[\{i,j,k\}\text{ filled in }\Cech(n,r,d) \;\big|\; \text{all three edges present}\bigr].
\]
\end{definition}

We set $q = \fillingP(p,d)$ so that both models share the same marginal fill probability.
Under this matching, any distributional difference in $\tauf$ reflects geometric dependence,
not marginal mismatch.

\begin{definition}[geometricCov]\label{def:geomcov}
The \emph{geometric covariance} $\geomCov(p,d)$ is the per-triangle contribution to
$\EE[\tauf]$ under $\Cech(n,r,d)$ with matched parameters:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:geomcov-def}
  \geomCov(p,d)
  = \EE_{\Cech}\!\bigl[(A_{ij}-p)(A_{ik}-p)(A_{jk}-p)(F_{ijk}-q)\bigr],
\end{equation}
where the expectation is over a single triangle $\{i,j,k\}$ drawn uniformly under
$\Cech(n,r,d)$ with $r = r(p,d)$ and $q = \fillingP(p,d)$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Asymptotic Properties of the Matched Parameters}

\begin{lemma}[Radius asymptotics]\label{lem:radius-asymptotics}
Fix $p\in(0,1)$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $r(p,d) = p^{1/d}/2\to 1/2$ as $d\to\infty$; the matched radius increases
  monotonically toward the torus diameter.
  \hfill \leanverified{matchRadius\_tendsto\_half}
\item $\geomCov(p,d)\to 0$ as $d\to\infty$.
  \hfill \leanverified{geometricCov\_tendsto\_zero}
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\textit{Part~(a).} Since $p\in(0,1)$, we have $\log p < 0$, so
$\log r(p,d) = (\log p)/d - \log 2 \to -\log 2$ as $d\to\infty$, giving $r(p,d)\to 1/2$.
Monotonicity holds because $p^{1/d}$ is increasing in $d$ for $p\in(0,1)$.

\textit{Part~(b).} The critical dimension $d^*(p) = |\log p|/\log(3/2)$ is finite.
We claim that for all integer $d \geq \lceil d^*(p)\rceil$, $\geomCov(p,d) = 0$ exactly;
the limit follows immediately since the sequence is eventually zero.

To see this, fix $d \geq d^*(p)$. Then $d \log(3/2) \geq |\log p| = \log(1/p)$, so
$(3/2)^d \geq 1/p$, giving $p^{1/d} \geq 2/3$ and $r(p,d) = p^{1/d}/2 \geq 1/3$.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:helly} below, any three balls of radius $r \geq 1/3$ on $\TT^d$ have
nonempty triple intersection, so every triple of points fills: $\fillingP(p,d) = 1$.
Therefore $q = 1$, giving $F_{ijk} - q = 0$ identically, and
\[
  \geomCov(p,d) = \EE_{\Cech}\!\bigl[(A_{ij}-p)(A_{ik}-p)(A_{jk}-p)\cdot 0\bigr] = 0. \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

The key geometric input is the following 1D Helly-type lemma.

\begin{lemma}[Three-arc intersection]\label{lem:helly}
Let $r \geq 1/3$ and let $x_1,x_2,x_3\in\TT^d$ be arbitrary. Then
$B(x_1,r)\cap B(x_2,r)\cap B(x_3,r)\neq\emptyset$.
\hfill \leanverified{addCircle\_three\_balls\_intersect'}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By the product structure $\TT^d = (\AddCircle)^d$, it suffices to prove the statement in
each coordinate separately: three sup-norm balls in $\TT^d$ intersect iff their projections
onto each $\AddCircle$-coordinate intersect. So it suffices to treat the case $d = 1$.

On $\AddCircle = \RR/\ZZ$ with the sup-norm, the ball $B(x_i,r)$ is a closed arc of length
$2r$, and its complement $B(x_i,r)^c$ is an open arc of length $1-2r$.

\textit{Case $r > 1/3$:} The three open complements have lengths $1-2r < 1/3$ each, with
total length $3(1-2r) < 1$. Since they cannot cover $\AddCircle$ (circumference~1), there
exists $y \notin \bigcup_i B(x_i,r)^c$, i.e., $y \in \bigcap_i B(x_i,r)$.

\textit{Case $r = 1/3$:} The three complements have length exactly $1/3$ each. If they do
not cover $\AddCircle$, a common point of the three balls exists as above. If they do cover
$\AddCircle$, they must be pairwise disjoint open arcs forming a partition (up to finitely
many boundary points). Denote the three boundary points (endpoints of the complementary arcs)
by $c_1, c_2, c_3$. Each $c_j$ is an endpoint of exactly two complementary arcs, hence lies
in the closure of two of the balls and in the interior of the third. In particular,
$c_1, c_2, c_3 \in B(x_1, 1/3) \cap B(x_2, 1/3) \cap B(x_3, 1/3)$, so the triple
intersection is nonempty.

In both cases, $\bigcap_i B(x_i, r) \neq \emptyset$.
\end{proof}

\section{The Doubly-Signed Statistic}\label{sec:statistic}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Definition}

Let $A_{ij}\in\{0,1\}$ denote the indicator that edge $\{i,j\}$ is present, and let
$F_{ijk}\in\{0,1\}$ denote the indicator that triangle $\{i,j,k\}$ is filled (zero if any
edge is absent). Fix parameters $p,q$ from the null model.

\begin{definition}[Doubly-signed statistic]\label{def:tauf}
The \emph{doubly-signed filled triangle statistic} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tauf-def}
  \tauf = \sum_{\{i,j,k\}\in\binom{[n]}{3}}
    \Bigl[\prod_{e\in\partial\{i,j,k\}}(A_e - p)\Bigr](F_{ijk} - q),
\end{equation}
where $\partial\{i,j,k\} = \bigl\{\{i,j\},\{i,k\},\{j,k\}\bigr\}$ denotes the three
boundary edges.
\end{definition}

The factor $\prod_{e\in\partial t}(A_e - p)$ is the BDER signed edge product for
triangle~$t$; the factor $(F_t - q)$ centers the fill indicator. The product is doubly
centered, vanishing in expectation whenever any of the four indicators is independent of the
rest. We write $Z_t = (A_{ij}-p)(A_{ik}-p)(A_{jk}-p)(F_{ijk}-q)$ for the per-triangle
summand, so $\tauf = \sum_t Z_t$.

\subsection{Moments Under \texorpdfstring{$\PC$}{2PC}}

\begin{proposition}[Moments under 2PC]\label{prop:moments-twopc}
Under $\PC(n,p,q)$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $\EE[\tauf] = 0$.
\item $\Var[\tauf] = \binom{n}{3}\cdot p^3(1-p)^3\cdot q(1-q)$.
  In particular, $\Var[\tauf\mid\PC] = \Theta(n^3)$.
\end{enumerate}
\hfill \leanverified{moments\_twoParam\_signed}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\textit{Part~(a).} Under $\PC$, all four indicators $A_{ij}, A_{ik}, A_{jk}, F_{ijk}$ are
mutually independent. Hence
\[
  \EE[Z_t] = \EE[A_{ij}-p]\cdot\EE[A_{ik}-p]\cdot\EE[A_{jk}-p]\cdot\EE[F_{ijk}-q] = 0,
\]
since $\EE[A_{ij}-p] = 0$. Summing over all $\binom{n}{3}$ triangles gives $\EE[\tauf]=0$.

\textit{Part~(b), off-diagonal terms.} For distinct triangles $t\neq t'$, we show
$\EE[Z_t Z_{t'}] = 0$. Under $\PC$, all edge and fill indicators are mutually independent.
Any edge $e \in \partial t \setminus \partial t'$ that appears in $t$ but not $t'$ contributes
an independent factor $(A_e - p)$ to $Z_t$ which is independent of $Z_{t'}$; since
$\EE[A_e-p]=0$, the product $\EE[Z_t Z_{t'}]=0$. The same argument applies if $t,t'$
share all three edges (impossible for distinct triangles) or if the fill $F_t$ is unshared.
More precisely: whenever any indicator in the product $Z_t Z_{t'}$ appears in exactly one of
$Z_t, Z_{t'}$, it contributes a mean-zero factor independent of the rest, killing the
expectation. One checks case by case that for any two distinct triangles (sharing 0, 1, 2, or
3 vertices), at least one such unshared indicator exists.

\textit{Part~(b), diagonal terms.} By mutual independence in $\PC$,
\[
  \Var[Z_t] = \EE[Z_t^2]
  = \EE[(A_{ij}-p)^2]\cdot\EE[(A_{ik}-p)^2]\cdot\EE[(A_{jk}-p)^2]\cdot\EE[(F_{ijk}-q)^2]
  = p(1-p)\cdot p(1-p)\cdot p(1-p)\cdot q(1-q).
\]
Since the $\binom{n}{3}$ diagonal terms are uncorrelated, summing gives
$\Var[\tauf] = \binom{n}{3}\cdot p^3(1-p)^3 q(1-q)$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Moments Under the \v{C}ech Model}

\begin{proposition}[Moments under \v{C}ech]\label{prop:moments-cech}
Under $\Cech(n,r,d)$ with $r = r(p,d)$ and matched $q = \fillingP(p,d)$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $\EE[\tauf] = \binom{n}{3}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)$.
  \hfill \leanverified{moments\_cech\_signed}
\item $\Var[\tauf] = O(n^4)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\textit{Part~(a).}
Write $Z_t = (A_{ij}-p)(A_{ik}-p)(A_{jk}-p)(F_{ijk}-q)$ for triangle $t = \{i,j,k\}$.
Under the \v{C}ech model, the joint law of $(A_{ij}, A_{ik}, A_{jk}, F_t)$ is determined by
the triple $(x_i,x_j,x_k)$ drawn uniformly on $(\TT^d)^3$. By definition of
$\geomCov(p,d)$~\eqref{eq:geomcov-def}, we have $\EE[Z_t] = \geomCov(p,d)$ for each
triangle. Since all $\binom{n}{3}$ triangles are identically distributed,
$\EE[\tauf] = \binom{n}{3}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)$.

\textit{Part~(b).}
Write $\tauf = \sum_t Z_t$ where $|Z_t|\leq 1$ always (since each factor is bounded by~1).

\smallskip
\noindent\textit{Diagonal terms.} $\Var[Z_t]\leq\EE[Z_t^2] \leq 1$ per triangle;
summing over $\binom{n}{3} = O(n^3)$ triangles gives $O(n^3)$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textit{Off-diagonal terms: no shared vertices.} For triangles $t, t'$ sharing no
vertex, the points $(x_i,x_j,x_k)$ and $(x_{i'},x_{j'},x_{k'})$ are independent (six i.i.d.\
uniform points), so $Z_t$ and $Z_{t'}$ are independent. Hence $\Cov(Z_t,Z_{t'}) = 0$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textit{Off-diagonal terms: exactly one shared vertex.} Suppose $t = \{i,j,k\}$
and $t' = \{i,j',k'\}$ share only vertex $i$, where $\{j,k\}\cap\{j',k'\}=\emptyset$.
Condition on $x_i$. Given $x_i$, the remaining points $(x_j,x_k)$ for $t$ and
$(x_{j'},x_{k'})$ for $t'$ are drawn uniformly and independently; in particular, $Z_t$ and
$Z_{t'}$ are conditionally independent given $x_i$. Therefore
\[
  \Cov(Z_t,Z_{t'}) = \EE[\Cov(Z_t,Z_{t'}\mid x_i)] + \Cov(\EE[Z_t\mid x_i],\EE[Z_{t'}\mid x_i]).
\]
The first term vanishes (conditional independence). For the second term, we claim
$\EE[Z_t\mid x_i]$ is constant in $x_i$: by the translation invariance of the uniform
measure on $\TT^d$, the conditional law of $(x_j - x_i, x_k - x_i) \pmod{1}$ is
uniform on $(\TT^d)^2$ regardless of $x_i$, so the distribution of $Z_t$ given $x_i$
does not depend on $x_i$, giving $\EE[Z_t\mid x_i] = \EE[Z_t]$. The same holds for
$\EE[Z_{t'}\mid x_i]$. Therefore $\Cov(\EE[Z_t\mid x_i],\EE[Z_{t'}\mid x_i]) = 0$,
and $\Cov(Z_t,Z_{t'}) = 0$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textit{Off-diagonal terms: one shared edge.} The only nontrivial contribution
comes from the $O(n^4)$ pairs of triangles sharing a common edge. For $t=\{i,j,k\}$ and
$t'=\{i,j,k'\}$ (sharing edge $\{i,j\}$), condition on $(x_i,x_j)$. The points $x_k$ and
$x_{k'}$ are then independent of each other and uniform on $\TT^d$. Define
\[
  g(x_i,x_j) = \EE_{x_k}[(A_{ik}-p)(A_{jk}-p)(F_{ijk}-q)\mid x_i,x_j],
\]
so that $\EE[Z_t \mid x_i,x_j] = (A_{ij}-p)\cdot g(x_i,x_j)$, and similarly for $t'$.
Since $|g|\leq 1$, we have $|\Cov(Z_t,Z_{t'})|\leq \Var_{(x_i,x_j)}[(A_{ij}-p)g(x_i,x_j)]
= O(1)$ per pair.

Combining: $\Var[\tauf] = O(n^3) + 0 + O(n^4)\cdot O(1) = O(n^4)$.
\end{proof}

\section{Detection Analysis}\label{sec:detection}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{lemma}[Signal-to-noise bound]\label{lem:snr}
With matched parameters $r = r(p,d)$ and $q = \fillingP(p,d)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:snr}
  \mathrm{SNR}(\tauf)
  \;=\; \frac{\bigl(\EE_{\Cech}[\tauf]-\EE_{\PC}[\tauf]\bigr)^2}{\Var_{\PC}[\tauf]}
  \;=\; \binom{n}{3}\cdot\frac{\geomCov(p,d)^2}{p^3(1-p)^3 q(1-q)}.
\end{equation}
In particular, $\mathrm{SNR}\to\infty$ if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:detection-cond}
  n^{3/2}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Propositions~\ref{prop:moments-twopc}(a) and~\ref{prop:moments-cech}(a),
$\EE_{\Cech}[\tauf]-\EE_{\PC}[\tauf] = \binom{n}{3}\geomCov(p,d)$ and
$\Var_{\PC}[\tauf] = \binom{n}{3}p^3(1-p)^3 q(1-q)$. Substituting gives~\eqref{eq:snr}.
Since $\binom{n}{3}=\Theta(n^3)$, $\mathrm{SNR}\to\infty$ iff $n^3\geomCov(p,d)^2\to\infty$,
i.e.,~\eqref{eq:detection-cond}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Detection Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Detection lower bound]\label{thm:detection}
Fix $p\in(0,1)$ and let $q = \fillingP(p,d)$. Suppose $d$ is fixed and
$n^{3/2}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty$ as $n\to\infty$.
Then the test that rejects~$\PC$ when $\tauf$ exceeds a suitable threshold achieves
asymptotic power~$1$:
\[
  \TV\bigl(\PC(n,p,q),\,\Cech(n,r(p,d),d)\bigr)\to 1.
\]
\hfill \leanverified{phase\_transition}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $g = \geomCov(p,d) > 0$ (positive since $d < d^*(p)$ when $d$ is fixed and $g > 0$),
and let $\mu_C = \EE_{\Cech}[\tauf] = \binom{n}{3}g$.
Set the threshold at $\theta = \mu_C/2$.

\textit{2PC side: probability above threshold goes to~0.}
By Chebyshev's inequality and Proposition~\ref{prop:moments-twopc},
\[
  \PP_{\PC}\!\left[\tauf > \theta\right]
  \;\leq\; \PP_{\PC}\!\left[|\tauf| > \theta\right]
  \;\leq\; \frac{\Var_{\PC}[\tauf]}{\theta^2}
  = \frac{\binom{n}{3}p^3(1-p)^3 q(1-q)}{\bigl(\frac{1}{2}\binom{n}{3}g\bigr)^2}
  = \frac{4p^3(1-p)^3 q(1-q)}{\binom{n}{3}g^2}.
\]
Since $d$ is fixed, $g>0$ is a positive constant, so $\binom{n}{3}g^2 = \Omega(n^3)\to\infty$.
Therefore $\PP_{\PC}[\tauf > \theta] \to 0$.

\textit{\v{C}ech side: probability above threshold goes to~1.}
We apply the Paley--Zygmund inequality: for any nonnegative random variable $W$,
$\PP[W > 0] \geq (\EE W)^2/\EE[W^2]$. Apply this with $W = (\tauf - \theta)_+$, using
that $\tauf \geq \theta$ implies $W = \tauf - \theta \geq 0$. More directly, the
Paley--Zygmund inequality gives
\[
  \PP_{\Cech}\!\left[\tauf > \tfrac{1}{2}\mu_C\right]
  \geq \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{\mu_C^2}{\EE_{\Cech}[\tauf^2]}.
\]
The numerator is $\mu_C^2 = \binom{n}{3}^2 g^2 = \Omega(n^6 g^2)$. For the denominator,
$\EE_{\Cech}[\tauf^2] = (\EE_{\Cech}[\tauf])^2 + \Var_{\Cech}[\tauf] \leq \mu_C^2 + Cn^4$
by Proposition~\ref{prop:moments-cech}(b). Therefore
\[
  \PP_{\Cech}\!\left[\tauf > \tfrac{1}{2}\mu_C\right]
  \geq \frac{c\,n^6 g^2}{n^6 g^2 + C n^4}
  = \frac{c}{1 + C/(n^2 g^2)}.
\]
Since $d$ is fixed and $g>0$ is a positive constant, $n^2g^2\to\infty$, giving the
denominator $\to 1$ and the lower bound $\to c > 0$. A more careful estimate shows the
bound tends to~1: rewrite as $1 - Cn^4/(n^6g^2+Cn^4) = 1 - o(1)$ since $n^2g^2\to\infty$.

Since $\PP_{\PC}[\tauf > \theta]\to 0$ and $\PP_{\Cech}[\tauf > \theta]\to 1$ for the same
threshold, the total variation between the laws of $\tauf$ tends to~1. Since
$\TV(\PC,\Cech) \geq \TV(\mathrm{law}_{\PC}(\tauf), \mathrm{law}_{\Cech}(\tauf))$, we
obtain $\TV(\PC, \Cech)\to 1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:detection-condition}
The Paley--Zygmund argument above uses $d$ fixed (so $g > 0$ is a constant independent of
$n$), which ensures $n^2g^2\to\infty$ and the \v{C}ech tail probability tends to~1.
In the simultaneous limit $n,d\to\infty$ with $\geomCov(p,d)\to 0$, the argument requires
the stronger condition $n\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty$ (so that $n^2g^2\to\infty$),
which is implied by the stated $n^{3/2}g\to\infty$ only when $g$ is not too small relative
to $n^{-1/2}$. Pinning down the exact decay rate of $\geomCov(p,d)$ as $d\nearrow d^*(p)$
is an open problem (see Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Phase Transition}

\begin{definition}[Critical dimension]\label{def:critical-dim}
Define the critical dimension
\begin{equation}\label{eq:critical-dim}
  d^*(p) \;=\; \frac{|\log p|}{\log(3/2)},
\end{equation}
the threshold at which the matched radius $r(p,d) = p^{1/d}/2$ crosses $1/3$.
Equivalently, $d^*(p)$ is the infimum of $d > 0$ such that $p^{1/d} \geq 2/3$,
i.e., $r(p,d) \geq 1/3$.
\end{definition}

With $\log(3/2)\approx 0.405$, the threshold is $d^*(p)\approx 2.47\,|\log p|$.
For example: $p=0.1$ gives $d^*(p)\approx 5.7$ (detection only for $d\leq 5$);
$p=0.01$ gives $d^*(p)\approx 11.4$ (only for $d\leq 11$);
more generally $p=10^{-k}$ gives $d^*(p)\approx 5.68k$.
Crucially, $d^*(p)$ depends only on $p$, not on $n$.

\begin{theorem}[Phase transition]\label{thm:phase-transition}
Fix $p\in(0,1)$ with $q = \fillingP(p,d)$ matched.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \textit{Detectable regime.} For any fixed $d < d^*(p)$ and any sequence $n\to\infty$,
  $\TV(\PC,\Cech)\to 1$. (Since $d < d^*(p)$ implies $\geomCov(p,d)>0$ is a positive
  constant, the condition $n^{3/2}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty$ is automatic.)
\item \textit{Collapsed regime.} For $d \geq d^*(p)$: $\geomCov(p,d) = 0$ exactly,
  $\tauf \equiv 0$ identically under both models, and $\TV(\PC,\Cech) = 0$.
\hfill \leanverified{geometricCov\_eventually\_zero}
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textit{Part~(a).} For $d < d^*(p)$, by definition we have $r(p,d) < 1/3$, so
$\fillingP(p,d) < 1$ and $q \in (0,1)$. Moreover, $\geomCov(p,d) > 0$ because the joint
distribution of $(A_{ij}, A_{ik}, A_{jk}, F_{ijk})$ under the \v{C}ech model is genuinely
different from the product distribution: not all triples fill, so the fill indicator carries
information beyond the edge indicators. Since $\geomCov(p,d) > 0$ is a fixed positive
constant, $n^{3/2}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty$ automatically, and Theorem~\ref{thm:detection}
gives $\TV(\PC,\Cech)\to 1$.

\textit{Part~(b).} For $d \geq d^*(p)$, the proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:radius-asymptotics}(b)
gives $r(p,d) \geq 1/3$, and Lemma~\ref{lem:helly} gives $\fillingP(p,d) = 1$.
Therefore $q = 1$, and $F_{ijk} - q = F_{ijk} - 1 = 0$ identically (since $F_{ijk}\in\{0,1\}$
and every triple fills). Hence every summand $Z_t = \cdots\cdot(F_{ijk}-1) = 0$, giving
$\tauf = 0$ under both models. In particular,
$\EE_{\Cech}[\tauf] = \EE_{\PC}[\tauf] = 0$ and $\TV = 0$, so no test can distinguish
the models.
\end{proof}

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Comparison with BDER}

The connection to Bubeck, Ding, Eldan, and R\'acz~\cite{Bubeck2016} is the central organizing
principle of this work. BDER established that the signed triangle statistic detects latent
$d$-dimensional geometry in a random graph when $d\ll n^3$, and the test fails when $d\gg n^3$
because edges become nearly independent in high dimensions---the geometric correlations in the
BDER covariance $\mathrm{cov}_{ij,ik}$ vanish as $d\to\infty$ but remain positive for every
finite $d$.

Our $\tauf$ is the simplicial extension: we add a second layer of centering for the fill
indicator. The geometric covariance $\geomCov(p,d)$ plays the role of $\mathrm{cov}_{ij,ik}$.
The detection condition $n^{3/2}\cdot\geomCov(p,d)\to\infty$ is structurally analogous to
BDER's condition (both require SNR $\to\infty$). The key qualitative difference is in
the nature of the threshold: BDER's threshold $d^*\asymp n^3$ grows without bound in $n$,
because $\mathrm{cov}_{ij,ik}(d)\sim C/\sqrt{d}$ is positive for all $d$ and the SNR
condition $n^3\,\mathrm{cov}^2\to\infty$ determines the critical~$d$. By contrast, our
threshold $d^*(p)$ is \emph{finite and independent of $n$}: $\geomCov(p,d) = 0$ exactly for
all $d \geq d^*(p)$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:phase-transition}(b),
\lean{geometricCov\_eventually\_zero}), so the signal collapses completely above the threshold
regardless of~$n$.

This sharp collapse is specific to the \v{C}ech fill structure on the sup-norm torus. In high
enough dimensions, ball intersections become universal on $\TT^d$ (by Lemma~\ref{lem:helly}:
once $r(p,d) \geq 1/3$, any three sup-norm balls on each $\AddCircle$-coordinate share a
common point), eliminating all geometric signal. In the graph-only regime (ignoring fills),
the problem reduces to BDER with the same $n^3$-type threshold; the simplicial fill layer
changes the detection problem fundamentally by introducing the finite phase transition.

\subsection{Limitations}

\paragraph{Flat torus geometry.}
This paper works on the flat torus $\TT^d$ with the sup-norm metric, for which the ball
volume formula $(2r)^d$ gives an explicit, algebraically tractable matched radius
$r(p,d) = p^{1/d}/2$. The sphere $\Sph^{d-1}$---the geometry used by BDER---would require
replacing this formula with the incomplete beta function, leading to a transcendental
matched-radius equation and substantially harder Lean formalization. The proof strategy
(doubly-signed statistic, phase transition argument) is morally the same on the sphere, but
porting the formalization would require redeveloping the volume matching, the filling
probability asymptotics, and the dominated convergence argument in a different geometry.
We therefore treat flat torus geometry as a fixed assumption, and leave sphere extensions
to future work.

\paragraph{One-sided detection.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:phase-transition}(b) shows the doubly-signed test is powerless for
$d \geq d^*(p)$, but does not show that \emph{all} tests fail. The full indistinguishability
statement requires bounding the second moment of the likelihood ratio---a combinatorial
problem harder than the graph case due to overlapping-triangle interactions, analogous to
Section~6 of BDER~\cite{Bubeck2016}.

\subsection{Future Work}

\paragraph{Detection rate below the threshold.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:phase-transition}(a) gives a sufficient condition for detection when
$d < d^*(p)$ with $d$ fixed. Pinning down the exact decay rate of $\geomCov(p,d)$ as
$d\nearrow d^*(p)$ (equivalently, as $r\nearrow 1/3$) requires precise asymptotics for triple
ball intersections on $\TT^d$ near the critical radius, a codimension-1 integral problem.
The low-degree polynomial framework of Liu, Mohanty, Schramm, and Yang~\cite{Liu2022} provides
a model for obtaining sharp detection thresholds in this regime; extending it to the
simplicial setting is a natural next step.

\paragraph{Higher-dimensional simplices.}
The analysis extends conceptually to $k$-simplices for $k\geq 3$: define a multiply-signed
statistic $\tau_{(k)}$ centering all $\binom{k+1}{2}$ edge indicators and the fill indicator.
The detection condition becomes $n^{(k+1)/2}\cdot\geomCov_k(p,d)\to\infty$, and each
increase in simplex dimension lowers the threshold exponent. The Lean formalization of
$k=2$ provides a template for higher~$k$.

\paragraph{Sphere geometry.}
Extending the results to points on $\Sph^{d-1}$ (the geometry studied by BDER) requires
new analysis of triple spherical cap intersections and a different matched-radius formula.
We leave this as a longer-term open problem.

\paragraph{Fourier and spectral methods.}
Bangachev and Bresler~\cite{BangachevBresler2024} showed that the signed triangle statistic
is computationally optimal for the graph testing problem via a low-degree Fourier analysis.
Extending this Fourier perspective to simplicial complexes---characterizing the Fourier
coefficients of the \v{C}ech complex distribution and showing optimality of $\tauf$ among
low-degree tests---would give a sharper understanding of the computational frontier in our
setting. Separately, the dense subhypergraph detection framework of Dhawan, Mao, and
Wein~\cite{DhawanMaoWein2023} addresses a combinatorial variant of the hyperedge detection
problem; the relationship to the geometric detection setting studied here remains to be
explored.

\item \lean{geometricCov\_eventually\_zero}: $\geomCov(p,d) = 0$ \emph{exactly}

\subsection*{Formalization Architecture}

The proof is organized as follows. Strategy~2 material begins at approximately line~634 of the
main file. The earlier lines (\textasciitilde1--630) contain legacy Strategy~1 material
(unsigned variance-gap approach) retained for reference. Strategy~1 was abandoned because
$\EE[V_f]\to L > 0$ as $d\to\infty$, so the unsigned statistic has nonzero mean under both
models in the limit; see the project's proof decisions log for the full history.

The Lean type conventions are as follows: the torus is
\texttt{Fin~d~$\to$~AddCircle~(1~:~$\mathbb{R}$)} with the sup-norm metric; edge
indicators are \texttt{Fin~n~$\to$~Fin~n~$\to$~Bool}; the measurable space on
\texttt{CechSample n d} is \texttt{MeasurableSpace.comap} \texttt{CechSample.points}
\texttt{inferInstance}. All Lean options match Aristotle's fixed environment (v4.28.0)
to ensure portability of returned proofs.
